## 1. WHY THE RENAME IS RIGHT (one paragraph, for the record)

The original naming logic — "radar = detection, ledger = the record" — named the **mechanism**. In this category, mechanism names read as surveillance no matter how the copy dances around it, because the mechanism *is* watching. The fix is structural, not cosmetic: name the **deliverable** (the brief) or the **outcome** (seeing your field, knowing where things are heading). Same rule governs every sentence on the site: the client is the subject, never the competitor.

## 3. THE REFRAME RULES

1. **The client is the subject.** Every sentence is about what *you* (the buyer) know, decide, and do — never about what we do *to* competitors.
2. **Name the deliverable, not the mechanism.** The product is a one-page brief, not a monitoring system.
3. **One confident provenance line, never a defensive essay.** Stated once, plainly, then move on.
4. **Every section ends in a next step.**

**Banned → replacement vocabulary (absolute, everywhere — site, emails, LinkedIn, briefs themselves):**

| Never | Instead |
|---|---|
| monitor / monitoring | your weekly brief, briefing, benchmarking |
| track / tracking | map, follow your market |
| surveillance, watch, radar, spy, recon | — (no replacement; delete the thought) |
| scraping | public-source research |
| gather intel on X | market intelligence (category noun only, never a verb) |
| competitor data | what's public in your market |
| we alert you about them | your brief flags what changed |
| trusted partner / we're your partner | — (never self-described; see below) |

**Partnership is shown, never said.** The word "partner" never appears in Obzio copy — every vendor claims it, so claiming it is vendor behavior. The perception is built by conduct the buyer can verify: evidence links on every claim, recommendations that sometimes say *do nothing*, willingness to disqualify bad-fit buyers in public copy, a guarantee that puts our credibility at risk before their money, and a named human signature on every brief. Prospects should reach the word "partner" themselves; the moment we say it first, we sound like everyone else.

**Signature convention:** every Obzio Brief closes with the reviewing analyst's name ("Reviewed by Ahmed Ibrahim"). A signed page reads as advice from a person; an unsigned PDF reads as a SaaS export.

**THE PROBLEM — Finding out late is expensive**

Three weeks ago your closest competitor cut their bundle price 17% and put fourteen new ads behind it. Your ROAS dipped. You blamed the algorithm, swapped creative, raised budgets. You found the real reason last night — at midnight, on their Instagram.

Every DTC founder already does competitive research. It looks like this: scrolling rivals' feeds after dinner, screenshotting ads at midnight, spotting a price change two weeks late, keeping it all in your head. No system. No memory. No answer on Monday to "what changed?"

Late isn't free. It's paid in blown ad spend, discounts you matched but never needed to, and launches you reacted to instead of saw coming. You already pay for market intelligence — you just pay it in margin.

---

**THE ARTIFACT — The Obzio Brief**

One page. Three sections. Monday, 7:00.

**What moved.** Every price change, new offer, creative push, and launch across your competitive set last week — each with its evidence link.
**The read.** Is that discount an acquisition push or a margin panic? Is the new creative angle working or flailing?
**The move.** One to three recommendations, specific to you. Some weeks the right move is *nothing* — knowing that is worth the price on its own.

When something big happens mid-week, you don't wait for Monday: a one-line alert lands the same day.

Every brief is checked and signed by the analyst who read your market that week — a name, not a no-reply.

> **FROM A RECENT BRIEF — supplements, IE** *(illustrative sample; live briefs carry evidence links)*
> **[Competitor] cut their 90-day bundle €89 → €74 on Tuesday and launched 14 new Meta ads, all price-led.**
> **The read:** their subscription price didn't move — this is a Q3 acquisition push, not a reprice. They're buying one-time buyers with margin.
> **The move:** don't match. Hold price, lead this week's creative with per-serving cost on subscription, and pick up their discount buyers at full price when they churn in 60 days.
